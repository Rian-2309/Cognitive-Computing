# 1️. Brain vs Mind

![https://img.jagranjosh.com/images/2022/December/22122022/what-is-the-difference-between-brain-and-mind.jpg](https://img.jagranjosh.com/images/2022/December/22122022/what-is-the-difference-between-brain-and-mind.jpg)
## Brain
- **Brain is a physical biological organ** inside the skull. It contains billions of neurons that communicate through **electrical and chemical** signals.
- It controls:
    - movement
    - emotions
    - thinking
    - memory
    - decision making.  
## Mind
- The **Mind** refers to the set of mental processes that arise from brain activity.
- These processes includes:
    - thinking
    - perception
    - imagination
    - reasoning
    - emotions
    - awareness

So, the **brain** is biological hardware, while the **mind** is the mental activity produced by that hardware.
Example: - When you solve a math problem: 
			- neurons in the brain fire
			- the mind performs reasoning

### Brain vs Mind (Comparison)

| Brain                                | Mind                      |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------------------- |
| Physical organ                       | Mental processes          |
| Made of neurons                      | Made of thoughts          |
| Can be seen and studied biologically | Cannot be seen directly   |
| Part of the body                     | Product of brain activity |

**Example: -** 
While driving:

# 3️. Problems Related to Mind (Mind–Body Problem)

![https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/35/Descartes_mind_and_body.gif|260](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/35/Descartes_mind_and_body.gif)

The **Mind–Body Problem** is a **philosophical question**:
>How do **mental processes (mind)** interact with the **physical brain and body**?

In other words:
>How do thoughts, emotions and consciousness arise from brain activity?
 
This question has been debated for centuries in **philosophy, psychology, neuroscience, and cognitive computing**.
To explain this, philosophers gave different theories-
### 1️. Dualism
Dualism says:
- Mind and body are two **separate entities**.
- Mind is **non-physical**.
- Brain and body are **physical**.
Famous Philosopher: - **René Descartes (1596–1650)**
Example:  
- Your thoughts are different from your physical body.
- Thinking and physical movement are different substances.
### 2. Physicalism (Materialism)
Physicalism says:
- Mind and body are not two **separate entities**.
- Mind is a **product of brain activity**.
- Consciousness arises from neurons and brain processes.
- No separate 'Soul' substance required.
**Mental States = brain activity**
Example:  
When neurons fire in the brain → **thoughts and emotions occur**.
### 3. Functionalism
Functionalism says:
- Mental states are defined by **what they do, not by what they are made of**.
This means:
- A mental process (mind) is identified by its function or role in a system, not by material.
#### Example:
Pain is defined not by brain chemistry but by its function: 
- detecting damage
- causing reaction
- producing avoidance behavior

Functionalism suggests that any system performing the same function (process information) could have a mind, including computers.
This idea strongly influenced **Artificial Intelligence** and **Cognitive Computing**.
Example:  
### Conclusion
| Theory        | Main Idea                              |
| ------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| Dualism       | Mind and body separate                 |
| Physicalism   | Mind = brain activity                  |
| Functionalism | Mind = information processing function |

# 4. Relationship Between Consciousness and Cognitive Computing
Cognitive computing tries to **simulate some functions of consciousness**, but it **does not create real consciousness**.
### Human Consciousness vs Cognitive Systems
| Human Consciousness  | Cognitive Computing     |
| -------------------- | ----------------------- |
| Real awareness       | Simulated awareness     |
| Has emotions         | No real emotions        |
| Self-aware           | Limited self-monitoring |
| Experiences feelings | Processes data          |
### Example:
When a **human doctor** diagnoses a disease:
- Understands symptoms
- Uses experience
- Feels empathy

When an **AI medical system** diagnoses:
- Analyzes medical data
- Uses machine learning models
- Suggests possible diseases

The system **acts intelligently**, but it is **not conscious like a human**.
## Why Consciousness is Important in Cognitive Computing
Researchers study consciousness to build systems that can:
- understand human behavior
- interact naturally with humans
- improve decision making
- process complex information

Examples:
- Virtual assistants
- Chatbots
- Medical decision systems
- Self-driving cars

# 5. Cognitive Workload?
Cognitive workload is the **amount of mental effort required to perform a task**.
Different tasks require different levels of mental processing 
### Levels of Cognitive Workload
| Situation                       | Level of Cognitive Workload |
| ------------------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Watching TV                     | Low                         |
| Reading a book                  | Medium                      |
| Solving a math problem          | High                        |
| Airplane pilot landing aircraft | Very High                   |
- If Cognitive Workload becomes too high, performance decreases.
- The more demanding a task is, the higher workload required to perform it effectively.
- When task is complex, then more cognitive workload required.
### Factor Influencing Cognitive Workload
- task complexity
- prior knowledge
- distractions
## Types of Cognitive Workload
![https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/%24s_%21FdEb%21%2Cf_auto%2Cq_auto%3Agood%2Cfl_progressive%3Asteep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F15c0ae1a-e57f-4cfd-9046-927a370d0bed_1050x700.png|368](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/%24s_%21FdEb%21%2Cf_auto%2Cq_auto%3Agood%2Cfl_progressive%3Asteep/https%3A%2F%2Fbucketeer-e05bbc84-baa3-437e-9518-adb32be77984.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F15c0ae1a-e57f-4cfd-9046-927a370d0bed_1050x700.png)
### 1. Intrinsic Cognitive Load
 Intrinsic cognitive load is the **mental effort required because of the complexity of the task or information itself.** 
 intrinsic = things that cannot be regenerated/ changed easily
#### Example
| Task                    | Intrinsic Load |
| ----------------------- | -------------- |
| 2 + 2                   | Very low       |
| Algebra equation        | Medium         |
| Quantum physics problem | Very high      |
### 2. Extraneous Cognitive Load
![https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/5b69a01ba2e409501de055d1/696a6039031594a1e19e635b_696a6038652b34ab5996755b_cognitive-load-theory-a-teachers-guide-infographic.webp|331](https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/5b69a01ba2e409501de055d1/696a6039031594a1e19e635b_696a6038652b34ab5996755b_cognitive-load-theory-a-teachers-guide-infographic.webp)
Extraneous cognitive load is the **extra mental effort caused by the way of information is presented (poor design or unnecessary information).**
extrinsic = things that can be regenerated/ modified
#### Example
| Situation                      | Extraneous Load |
| ------------------------------ | --------------- |
| Poorly designed website        | High            |
| Confusing lecture slides       | High            |
| Clear diagrams and explanation | Low             |
- If teacher poorly explain the complex topic, then the student do extra mental effort.
### 3. Germane Cognitive Load
 Germane cognitive load is the **mental effort used to understand and learn new information and building knowledge structures (schemas).**
#### Example
| Situation                   | Germane Load |
| --------------------------- | ------------ |
| Understanding concept       | High         |
| Creating mental connections | High         |
| Memorizing patterns         | Medium       |
When student:
- understand concept
- solve examples
- making connections between them
then the Brain makes a knowledge structure, this is Germane load.
### Comparison of Three Types
| Type       | Source                      | Can it be changed?  | Example               |
| ---------- | --------------------------- | ------------------- | --------------------- |
| Intrinsic  | Task complexity             | Difficult to change | Hard math problem     |
| Extraneous | Presentation of information | can be reduced      | Confusing slides      |
| Germane    | Learning process            | can be reduced      | Concept understanding |

# 6. Measuring Cognitive Workload
**Measuring cognitive workload** means **finding out how much mental effort a person is using during a task.**
Researchers measure it in different ways.
### A. Physiological Methods (Body Signals)
Scientists observe body signals such as:
- **Heart rate**
- **Brain activity (EEG)**
- **Eye movement**
- **Pupil dilation**
Example: - If a person is solving a hard problem, **brain activity increases**.

### B. Performance-Based Methods
Measure how well a person performs a task.
Indicators include:
- task completion time
- number of mistakes
- response speed
Example: - If a student makes many mistakes in a test → workload may be **too high**.

### C. Subjective Methods
People **report their own mental effort**.
Example tools:
- questionnaires 
- rating scales (NASA-TLX workload scale)

Example question:
> “How mentally difficult was this task?”

# 7. Mitigating Cognitive Workload

![https://boagworld.sirv.com/Images/Blog-Images/conversion-rate-optimisation-07.jpg?profile=Small|328](https://boagworld.sirv.com/Images/Blog-Images/conversion-rate-optimisation-07.jpg?profile=Small)
**Mitigating cognitive workload** means **reducing the mental effort required to complete a task**.
In simple words:
> **Making tasks easier for the brain.**

### Methods to Reduce Cognitive Workload

#### A. Simplify Information
Provide **clear and simple instructions**.
Example: - Simple app interface instead of complex menus.
#### B. Reduce Multitasking
Too many tasks at once increase workload.
Example: - Driving + texting → very high cognitive load.
#### C. Use Visual Aids
Images and diagrams help people understand information faster.
Example: - Charts instead of long text.
#### D. Automation
Computers perform repetitive tasks.
Example: - Auto spell-check in word processors.
